Q1
Scenario: In October 2023, a mid-size regional bank faces severe liquidity pressures after social-media rumours trigger rapid deposit withdrawals. The bank holds a portfolio of long-dated government bonds currently worth less than their book value due to rising interest rates, but its loan book is fundamentally sound and credit losses are minimal. The central bank's emergency lending facility is considering whether to provide support.

Which principle best justifies central bank intervention here, and what condition should the central bank insist upon?

Correct answer: C

The classic distinction in banking regulation is between illiquidity and insolvency. This bank suffers from illiquidity — bond prices fell due to rate rises, not credit default — while its loan book remains sound. The lender-of-last-resort doctrine prescribes lending freely at a penalty rate against good collateral to such institutions. The penalty rate discourages moral hazard (banks shouldn't treat emergency facilities as cheap financing), and "good collateral" protects the central bank if the bank ultimately fails.

Option A conflates unrealised losses with insolvency — mark-to-market solvency is not the test. Option B confuses the FDIC's resolution role with the central bank's emergency liquidity function. Option D is wrong for the same reason as A, and setting rates at market levels defeats the purpose of emergency support.
📌 Key concept: The GFC 2008 saw the Fed deploy facilities (TAF, TSLF, PDCF) lending over $1 trillion at peak — all consistent with this framework. SVB (2023) is a modern parallel of a bank destroyed by an illiquidity spiral despite a fundamentally sound loan book.
Q2
Scenario: Horizon Community Bank (assets: $3.2B) has just been declared insolvent. Two resolution options are on the table: (i) the FDIC directly pays all insured depositors up to $250,000 and liquidates assets; (ii) a larger regional bank, Apex Bank, agrees to assume all deposits — insured and uninsured — and takes over the branch network and loan book. The FDIC calculates that option (ii) costs it an additional $40M versus option (i).

Why might the FDIC nonetheless prefer the Purchase & Assumption (P&A) method despite the higher direct cost?

Correct answer: D — Contagion prevention.

The FDIC's primary mandate is not cost minimisation but financial stability. A payoff method — even if cheaper in isolation — can trigger a bank panic if uninsured depositors at other banks become fearful. The P&A method short-circuits this: deposits transfer seamlessly to Apex Bank and customers experience no disruption. The systemic cost of a spreading panic would dwarf the $40M difference.

Option A is wrong — P&A is typically more costly for the FDIC, as stated in the scenario. Option B has no regulatory basis. Option C is wrong — P&A here protects all depositors including uninsured ones, which actually increases moral hazard rather than reducing it.
📌 Key concept: This is why TBTF institutions almost always receive P&A treatment — their failure would create contagion effects that make direct cost comparisons irrelevant.
Q3
Scenario: A government expands deposit insurance from $100,000 to $250,000 per depositor. Over the next five years, analysts observe: (i) depositor withdrawals from high-risk banks decline sharply; (ii) several newly chartered banks rapidly accumulate risky, high-yield loan portfolios; (iii) traditional banks complain about unfair competition from these aggressive new entrants. A World Bank study finds the country experiences an increase in banking crises in the decade following the expansion.

Which combination of regulatory problems best explains observations (i), (ii), and (iii) respectively?

Correct answer: A

Each observation maps to a distinct information problem:

(i) Depositor withdrawals decline from risky banks → Moral hazard (depositor side). Insured depositors have no incentive to monitor risky banks — their funds are guaranteed regardless. Market discipline collapses.

(ii) New banks accumulate risky portfolios → Adverse selection (ownership side). Deposit insurance subsidises risk-taking, making banking attractive to those who intend to take on excessive risk. Risk-lovers self-select into banking precisely because the downside is socialised.

(iii) Traditional banks face unfair competition → Moral hazard (institutional side). New entrants can offer higher rates and take riskier positions because losses are insured, forcing conservative banks to compete on unequal footing or match the risk levels.
📌 Key concept: Demirgüç-Kunt & Detragiache (World Bank) found explicit deposit insurance is statistically linked to higher incidence of banking crises — because these combined effects erode market discipline entirely.
Q4

• Tier 1 Capital: $9.6 billion  • On-balance-sheet assets: $210 billion  • Off-balance-sheet exposures (converted): $30 billion

The Supplementary Leverage Ratio (SLR) = Tier 1 Capital ÷ (on-BS assets + off-BS conversions).

Significantly Under< 6%< 3%< 3%Mandatory controls on compensation & dividends
Critically Under< 2%—< 2%Receivership within 90 days unless waived

Calculate Sterling National Bank's SLR and identify its PCA capitalisation zone and regulatory implication.

Correct answer: B

Step 1 — Total Leverage Exposure: $210B + $30B = $240 billion
Step 2 — SLR: $9.6B ÷ $240B = 4.0%
Step 3 — PCA Zone: The Adequately Capitalised threshold for leverage ratio is ≥ 4%. At exactly 4.0%, Sterling sits right at the boundary — not yet Undercapitalised (which requires < 4%), but it cannot accept brokered deposits without a waiver, and a single adverse shock pushes it below the line into mandatory capital plan territory.

Option D is a common error: 4.0% is the threshold for Adequately Capitalised, not below it — so Undercapitalised is incorrect. Option A's SLR of 5.33% would require Total Leverage Exposure of ~$180B, which is wrong. Option C's SLR of 3.20% would require Tier 1 of ~$7.7B, also wrong.
📌 Key concept: The SLR is a non-risk-sensitive backstop applied to all assets equally. Basel III introduced it to prevent banks from gaming RWA calculations by loading up on nominally "low-risk" assets. Being exactly at the 4.0% boundary warrants serious supervisory attention.
Q5
Scenario: Albion Bank holds the following portfolio (Basel III Standardised Approach):
• U.S. Treasury bonds: $400M (0% risk weight)
• Residential mortgages, LTV < 80%: $600M (50% risk weight)
• Unrated corporate loans: $200M (100% risk weight)
• Speculative-grade loans: $100M (150% risk weight)

Albion holds $38M in CET1 capital. Minimum CET1 ratio = 4.5%.

What are Albion Bank's total Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA), and does the bank meet the minimum CET1 requirement?

Correct answer: C

RWA = asset × risk weight, summed across all classes:
• Treasuries: $400M × 0% = $0M
• Mortgages: $600M × 50% = $300M
• Corporate loans: $200M × 100% = $200M
• Speculative: $100M × 150% = $150M
Total RWA = $650M

CET1 Ratio = $38M ÷ $650M = 5.85% — meets the 4.5% minimum.

However, 5.85% falls below the 7.0% threshold (4.5% minimum + 2.5% conservation buffer), so Albion still faces restrictions on dividend and bonus distributions. Option A incorrectly applies risk weights to the full balance sheet. Option B omits speculative loans. Option D applies 100% risk weight to mortgages rather than 50%.
📌 Key concept: U.S. Treasuries carry a 0% risk weight — Albion's entire $400M Treasury position contributes nothing to RWA. This treatment effectively subsidises government borrowing and explains why banks accumulate large sovereign portfolios even at low yields.

Correct answer: A — Undercapitalised

A bank is classified by its worst ratio. Checking each against the table:
• Total Capital/RWA: 7.2% < 8% → Undercapitalised
• CET1/RWA: 4.1% < 4.5% → Undercapitalised
• Leverage Ratio: 3.8% < 4% → Undercapitalised

📌 Key concept: PCA was designed to prevent "regulatory forbearance" — the pre-FDICIA practice of delaying action on weak banks, which deepened the S&L crisis losses by allowing problems to compound unchecked for years.
Q7
Scenario: In 2028, Atlas Financial — a G-SIB (Global Systemically Important Bank: a designation assigned by the Financial Stability Board to institutions whose failure could destabilise the global financial system; current examples include JPMorgan, Citigroup, HSBC, and BNP Paribas) — is approaching insolvency following catastrophic derivatives losses. Its assets total $2.8 trillion.

The U.S. Treasury Secretary convenes an emergency meeting. One official argues: "Dodd-Frank's Orderly Liquidation Authority (OLA) gives us the tools — we should not bail out Atlas." Another argues: "Markets have priced in an implicit government guarantee for years. A disorderly failure will trigger a systemic crisis worse than 2008."

Which statement most accurately characterises the "Too Big to Fail" problem as it applies to this scenario?

Correct answer: D

The TBTF problem is fundamentally about credibility and market expectations. G-SIBs borrow at a discount to peers because markets assume they will be rescued. An abrupt reversal of that implicit guarantee creates its own systemic shock: creditors of other G-SIBs would immediately reprice their exposure, potentially triggering a crisis far broader than the original failure.

Option A overstates Dodd-Frank — OLA is a legal mechanism, but the market's expectation of rescue is a behavioural reality that law alone cannot extinguish overnight. Option B confuses the $700B threshold (Basel III Endgame applicability) with TBTF, which is about systemic importance, not a hard asset cutoff. Option C is practically impossible and legally incoherent.
📌 Key concept: This is why TBTF is described as "a moral hazard that worsens the instability regulators sought to prevent" — the implicit guarantee encourages greater risk-taking by G-SIBs, making future crises more likely and more costly.
Q8
Scenario: In early 2023, Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) collapsed rapidly. Post-mortem analysis showed SVB had accumulated a large portfolio of long-duration AFS securities (Available-For-Sale securities: bonds and other fixed-income instruments held on the balance sheet that are marked to market each quarter, with unrealised gains and losses recorded in equity) whose market value had fallen sharply as interest rates rose. Because SVB fell below the $700B asset threshold for advanced approaches, it was not required to include these unrealised losses — known as AOCI (Accumulated Other Comprehensive Income) — in its reported CET1 capital. Its CET1 ratio therefore appeared healthy to outside observers.

Following SVB's collapse, the Fed, OCC, and FDIC jointly published an NPR (Notice of Proposed Rulemaking: a formal regulatory document that sets out proposed new rules for public consultation before they become law) on July 27, 2023, proposing to extend AOCI inclusion in CET1 to all banks with $100B+ in assets.

What fundamental regulatory failure does the SVB case illustrate, and why would mandatory AOCI inclusion have been an effective early-warning mechanism?

Correct answer: A

SVB is a textbook example of a regulatory threshold creating a perverse incentive. Its $15B+ in unrealised AFS losses were invisible to its reported capital ratios. Had AOCI been included, SVB's CET1 ratio would have been dramatically lower — likely triggering PCA scrutiny or restrictions months before the deposit run, giving supervisors time to act before a crisis materialised.

Option B is incorrect — AOCI is a mark-to-market interest rate risk measure, not a leverage metric; reducing assets would not have addressed the bond portfolio problem. Option C is partly true (deposit insurance played a role in the aftermath) but doesn't address the AOCI regulatory mechanism. Option D confuses SVB's bond duration problem entirely with the GFC-era originate-to-distribute securitisation model.
📌 Key concept: SVB's March 2023 failure directly validated the NPR's reform agenda. The fact that the NPR was published just four months after SVB's collapse — with AOCI inclusion as a centrepiece — confirms that supervisors viewed the threshold exemption as a critical regulatory gap.
Q9
Scenario: In September 2008, Reserve Primary Fund — the oldest U.S. money market mutual fund — "broke the buck" when its NAV fell below $1 per share due to $785M in Lehman Brothers exposure. Within 72 hours, over $310 billion in MMF outflows occurred industry-wide. The U.S. Treasury intervened with an emergency guarantee for all MMF deposits. By 2023, U.S. MMF assets had grown to $4.6 trillion — larger than pre-2008 levels.

Which argument best explains why the MMF systemic risk is arguably worse today than in 2008, and what structural feature makes MMFs inherently vulnerable to runs?

Correct answer: C

MMFs' structural fragility stems from a fundamental mismatch: they promise capital stability (the $1 NAV) while investing in assets whose values can fluctuate. This creates a first-mover advantage run incentive — the first to redeem gets par value, incentivising all investors to run simultaneously at the first hint of trouble — exactly what happened in 2008.

The systemic concern is larger today because: (1) MMF assets are now $4.6T; (2) the 2008 Treasury emergency guarantee created an implicit backstop, adding moral hazard; (3) MMFs are deeply embedded in short-term funding — their withdrawal freezes commercial paper and repo markets.

Option A is wrong — MMFs are not FDIC-insured. Option B overstates the 2014 reform: it applied only to institutional prime funds; government and retail MMFs retained the fixed $1 NAV. Option D also overstates the 2023 reforms — fees reduce runs at the margin but don't eliminate the structural incentive, as the 2020 Covid stress episode confirmed.
📌 Key concept: Shadow banking — of which MMFs are a major component — lies largely outside the Basel framework. The 2008 emergency guarantee means MMFs now carry an implicit government backstop without the formal regulatory obligations that accompany deposit insurance.
